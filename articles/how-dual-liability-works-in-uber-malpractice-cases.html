How Dual Liability Works In Uber Malpractice Cases

As a rideshare passenger, you trust that you will be safely transported from one location to another. However, accidents can still happen, and when they do, it's essential to understand the concept of dual liability in Uber malpractice cases.

What is Dual Liability?

Dual liability refers to the idea that both the Uber driver and the hospital or medical facility where you received treatment can be held responsible for any injuries or damages sustained during the incident. This legal doctrine recognizes that the chain of causation often involves more than one negligent party, and each party may bear a portion of the financial responsibility for the plaintiff‚??s losses.

In practice, dual liability can arise when the driver‚??s reckless behavior creates the accident, and the subsequent medical care fails to meet the accepted standard of care. Courts evaluate each party‚??s contribution to the overall harm, often applying comparative negligence rules to allocate fault proportionally. For example, if a driver is found 70% at fault for the crash and a hospital is 30% at fault for a misdiagnosis that worsened the injury, the settlement may be divided accordingly.

To establish dual liability in an Uber malpractice case, you will need to provide evidence of the following:

  • Documentation of the hospital or medical facility's failure to provide adequate care or treatment.
  • Medical records and expert testimony to support your claims of injury and damages.

Legal Framework and Precedents

The legal foundation for dual liability in rideshare and medical malpractice claims rests on two primary doctrines: vicarious liability and comparative negligence. Vicarious liability holds an entity (such as Uber) responsible for the actions of its independent contractors‚??drivers‚??when those actions occur within the scope of the service. Courts have increasingly applied this doctrine to rideshare platforms, especially after the California Supreme Court‚??s decision in Dynamex Operations LLC v. On the medical side, the doctrine of negligence requires the plaintiff to prove that the provider breached the standard of care, causing injury. Landmark cases such as Helling v. When both parties are implicated, the court typically employs a ‚??joint and several liability‚?ù approach, allowing the plaintiff to recover the full amount of damages from either defendant, who may then seek contribution from the other.
