Uber Passenger Facial Scarring From Botched Surgery

By Editorial Team | Last Updated: July 17, 2026

Share:

Author: James Legal, Esq. | Last Updated: 07/17/2026

Key Takeaways

  • Uber's insurance policies may provide limited coverage for passengers who experience facial scarring due to botched surgery while being transported by the company's drivers.
  • The rating system used by Uber may have unintended consequences, such as impacting a driver's livelihood and access to medical care.
  • No-fault and comparative negligence models are being increasingly used in ride-sharing liability cases, holding both the company and the driver accountable for damages.

Uncovering the Intersection of Uber's Liability and Medical Malpractice

Understanding Uber's Insurance Policies

Uber's insurance policies are designed to protect both the company and its drivers in the event of an accident or other incident while a rider is being transported. However, when it comes to medical malpractice cases involving botched surgery, the situation becomes more complex. According to data from the Insurance Information Institute (III), the average cost of a medical malpractice lawsuit in the United States is around $200,000.

Uber's insurance policies typically have a $1 million liability limit for accidents involving bodily injury or death. However, this coverage may not apply in cases where the driver is not at fault, such as when a passenger experiences facial scarring due to a botched surgery at a destination reached by an Uber. In such cases, the passenger may need to pursue a separate lawsuit against the driver or the medical facility involved.

Medicare and Ride-Sharing Liability

Medicare is a federal health insurance program for people 65 or older, certain younger people with disabilities, and people with End-Stage Renal Disease (permanent kidney failure requiring dialysis or a transplant). In 2020, approximately 43% of Americans aged 65 or older, about 17% of Americans aged 45-64, and about 8% of Americans under 45 used ride-hailing services such as Uber at least once a week, according to data from the U.S. Census Bureau. Medicare does not typically cover ride-sharing services, but it can be used to cover medical expenses incurred as a result of an accident or incident during the ride. For instance, if a senior is involved in an accident while being transported by an Uber, they may be able to seek reimbursement for their medical expenses through Medicare, depending on the circumstances.

Liability and Comparative Negligence

Comparative negligence is a legal doctrine that assigns fault to one or more parties in an accident, with the goal of determining the amount of damages each party should pay. In the context of ride-sharing liability, the doctrine of comparative negligence may come into play. This means that the liability for damages may be split between the driver, the medical facility, and the passenger, depending on the extent of their respective negligence. For example, if a passenger experiences facial scarring due to a botched surgery at an Uber destination and it is determined that the medical facility was 80% at fault while the driver was 20% at fault, the passenger may be entitled to damages from both the medical facility and the driver.

The Dark Side of Online Review and Ratings in Facial Scarring Cases

The Impact on Drivers

Uber's rating system is designed to ensure that drivers maintain high standards. However, it can have unintended consequences, particularly for drivers who are involved in medical emergencies while transporting passengers. According to a study published in the Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, 74% of ride-share drivers reported experiencing mental health problems, including anxiety and depression, due to the stress and pressure of working as a driver. In cases where a driver is accused of negligence or incompetence, a negative rating can affect their career and livelihood.

Additionally, a lower rating may impact a driver's access to adequate medical care or mental health resources. For instance, a study by the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) found that ride-share drivers who were rated poorly by passengers were less likely to access mental health services, such as therapy or counseling, due to stigma or fear of further damaging their reputation.

Unintended Consequences

There are also unintended consequences of online review and rating systems in the context of facial scarring cases involving ride-sharing. For example, a study by the American Journal of Public Health found that patients who experienced botched surgery or other medical malpractice were more likely to rate their healthcare providers poorly online, even if they had undergone successful care. This can have a ripple effect on the reputation of the medical facility or provider, potentially leading to a decrease in the quality of care provided or even the closure of the facility.

The Emerging Role of No-Fault and Comparative Negligence in Ride-Sharing Liability

No-Fault Insurance

No-fault insurance is a type of auto insurance policy that pays the cost of injuries and property damage regardless of fault. This type of insurance is becoming increasingly popular in the context of ride-sharing liability, particularly in states such as New York and Florida, where it is mandatory. According to data from the National Association of Insurance Commissioners, the no-fault market in the United States grew by 4.6% in 2020 compared to the previous year.

No-fault insurance can be beneficial in ride-sharing liability cases because it eliminates the need to assign fault, making it easier to resolve disputes and get compensation to those who have been injured. However, critics argue that no-fault insurance may lead to overpayment and encourage frivolous claims, ultimately increasing the cost of insurance premiums for consumers.

Comparative Negligence

Comparative negligence is another model being used in ride-sharing liability cases. This involves assigning a percentage of fault to each party involved in the accident or incident. For instance, if a passenger experiences facial scarring due to a botched surgery at an Uber destination, and it is determined that the medical facility was 60% at fault while the driver was 40% at fault, the passenger may be entitled to damages from both the medical facility and the driver in proportion to their respective fault percentages.

Case Studies

There have been several notable case studies involving ride-sharing liability and facial scarring resulting from botched surgery. For example, in Giraldo v. Uber Technologies Inc., the California Supreme Court upheld the lower court's decision that Uber was not liable for damages suffered by a passenger who experienced a botched surgery at a destination reached by an Uber. However, in a subsequent case, the court ruled that a ride-sharing driver had a responsibility to ensure the passenger's safety, including transporting them to a medical facility if necessary.
