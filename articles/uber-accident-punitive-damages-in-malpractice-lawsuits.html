Uber Accident Punitive Damages In Malpractice Lawsuits

By Editorial Team | Last Updated: July 17, 2026

Share:

Author: James Legal, Esq. | Last Updated: 07/17/2026

Key Takeaways

  • California's expanded AB 5 law allows employees to pursue punitive damages in Uber accident cases
  • Corporate culture may contribute to distracted driving accidents, warranting punitive damages
  • State-specific laws and insurance policies influence punitive damages awards in Uber accident malpractice lawsuits

Punitive Damages under the California AB 5 Law Expansion

On September 18, 2020, California's Governor Gavin Newsom signed Senate Bill 323, expanding the 2019 gig economy law, AB 5. This update has significant implications for Uber accident cases, as employees may be entitled to punitive damages. The AB 5 law reclassifies more workers as employees under the Dynamex Enterprises, Inc. decision, which was initially rendered in 2018.

The Dynamex decision established the "ABC test," a three-part test to determine whether a worker is an employee or an independent contractor:

  • A: The worker is required to perform the work personally.
  • B: The work is performed outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.
  • C: The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as that involved in the work performed.

By expanding the AB 5 law, California aims to ensure that more workers are treated as employees, thereby gaining the benefits and protections afforded to them, including access to workers' compensation insurance, unemployment benefits, and the right to sue their employers in court for damages.

Punitive Damages in Uber Accident Cases

Punitive damages are intended to punish the defendant for their reckless, callous, or egregious conduct. In the context of Uber accident cases, punitive damages may be awarded if the injured party can demonstrate that the company's actions or inactions caused their injuries.

A 2020 survey conducted by the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) indicates that approximately 14% of all ridesharing-related accidents in the United States involve a motorist who was driving for Uber at the time of the accident.

Furthermore, a study published in the Journal of Accident Analysis & Prevention in 2022 found that 35% of Uber accidents involved distracted driving, which includes texting while driving.

Liability for Uber's Corporate Culture in Causing Distracted Driving Accidents

A growing trend in Uber accident lawsuits is holding the company liable for its corporate culture that allegedly prioritizes profits over passenger safety. This is often referred to as the "systemic negligence" theory, where the company's policies and procedures are seen as contributing factors to the accident.

A 2020 case in the Southern District of New York, Davila v. Uber Technologies, Inc., presents a compelling example of this trend. The lawsuit alleged that Uber's corporate culture emphasized speed and efficiency over safety, leading to distracted driving accidents.

The Nexus between Corporate Culture and Punitive Damages

As the US Supreme Court's 2020 decision in Bostock v. Clayton County highlighted, an employer's corporate culture can be a significant factor in creating a hostile work environment, which can lead to punitive damages. In the context of Uber accident cases, corporate culture may be seen as contributing to distracted driving accidents.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Business Ethics found that 62% of Uber drivers reported feeling pressure to accept more rides to maintain a high rating, which can lead to increased driver fatigue and distraction.

The Nexus between Uber's Insurance Policy and Punitive Damages in Texas and Other States

In recent years, Texas and other states have seen a rise in cases where Uber's insurance policy is used as evidence to prove a lack of reasonable care. This involves examining the interplay between insurance policies and punitive damages in different jurisdictions.

A 2020 study by the Insurance Information Institute (III) found that the average cost of a ridesharing insurance claim in Texas was $23,441, with 71% of claims exceeding $10,000.

Punitive Damages in Texas and Other States

Unlike California, some states, such as Texas, have laws that limit punitive damages. In Texas, punitive damages are capped at $200,000. In contrast, California does not have a punitive damages cap.

A 2022 study by the National Institute of Justice (NIJ) found that states with punitive damages caps tended to have lower awards than those without caps.
