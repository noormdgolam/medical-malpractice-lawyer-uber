Who Pays For Medical Bills If A Surgeon Botches An Uber Accident Surgery?

Understanding Medical Malpractice and Dual Liability

As a medical malpractice attorney, I've seen countless cases where a botched surgery has left patients with devastating consequences. When an Uber accident surgery goes wrong, it's not uncommon for patients to wonder who will pay for their medical bills. In this article, we'll explore the complex issue of dual liability and provide actionable step-by-step advice on what to do in the first 24 hours.

Actionable Step-by-Step Advice (First 24 Hours)

  • Document Everything: Start by documenting every detail of your experience, including the accident, the surgery, and any subsequent medical treatment. Keep a record of all medical bills, correspondence with healthcare providers, and any communication with insurance companies.
  • Seek Medical Attention: If you haven't already, seek medical attention immediately. Even if you're not experiencing severe symptoms, it's essential to get checked out to prevent any potential complications.
  • Contact Your Insurance Company: Reach out to your insurance company to report the accident and the subsequent surgery. Make sure to provide them with all necessary documentation, including police reports and medical records.
  • Consult with a Medical Malpractice Attorney: Contact a medical malpractice attorney who specializes in Uber accident cases. They can help you navigate the complex process of dual liability and ensure you receive the compensation you deserve.

Evidentiary Requirements

To establish a successful medical malpractice claim, you'll need to provide evidence that demonstrates:

  • Standard of Care: Proof that the surgeon deviated from the accepted standard of care, resulting in your injury.
  • Causation: Evidence that the surgeon's actions directly caused your injury or worsened your condition.
  • Damages: Documentation of the extent of your injuries, including medical bills, lost wages, and any other related expenses.

Composite Case Study: Rideshare/Hospital Dual-Liability Settlement

Meet Jane Doe, a 35-year-old mother who was involved in a car accident while using Uber. She suffered severe injuries and underwent emergency surgery at a local hospital. Unfortunately, the surgeon botched the procedure, leaving Jane with permanent nerve damage and a lengthy rehabilitation process.

Jane's medical bills exceeded $500,000, and she was forced to take time off work to recover. Her insurance company initially denied her claim, citing a pre-existing condition. Undeterred, Jane consulted with a medical malpractice attorney who specialized in Uber accident cases.

With the attorney's help, Jane was able to establish dual liability against both the hospital and the Uber driver. The hospital was found to be 60% liable, while the Uber driver was 40% liable. Jane received a settlement of $1.2 million, which covered her medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

According to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS), Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) laws require Medicare to pay secondary to other insurance coverage, including liability insurance. This means that in cases like Jane's, Medicare may not be the primary payer, and other insurance companies may be responsible for covering her medical expenses.

The Journal of Patient Safety notes that medical errors are a leading cause of patient harm and death in the United States. It's essential for patients to advocate for themselves and seek medical attention immediately if they suspect a medical error has occurred.
