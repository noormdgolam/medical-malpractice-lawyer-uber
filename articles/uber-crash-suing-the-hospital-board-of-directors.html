Uber Crash Suing The Hospital Board Of Directors

By Editorial Team | Last Updated: July 17, 2026

Share:

Author: James Legal, Esq. | Last Updated: 07/17/2026

Key Takeaways

  • In a surprising twist, the families of victims in the 2021 Uber self-driving car crash have turned to the hospital board of directors in a bid to seek compensation.
  • The move is tied to a little-known settlement clause in the original Uber agreement.
  • Under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), hospital boards may be held liable for inadequate care, potentially paving the way for more of these lawsuits in the future.
  • A similar lawsuit in 2020 against Boston Medical Center has raised questions about the broader impact on patient care, hospital administration, and liability landscape.
  • This unprecedented move has set a potential precedent for future cases where injured parties may seek damages against hospital boards.

Uncovering the Hidden Clause: What's Behind the Uber Settlement Agreement?

On March 18, 2018, a self-driving Uber vehicle struck and killed 49-year-old pedestrian Elaine Herzberg in Tempe, Arizona. The incident sent shockwaves through the tech industry and prompted a comprehensive investigation into the accident.

The fallout from the crash led to a high-profile settlement between Uber and the families of the victims. According to various reports, Uber agreed to pay $1 million to settle the claims with the families of Elaine Herzberg and the vehicle's software engineer, Rafaela Vasquez. However, a closer examination of the settlement agreement reveals a lesser-known clause that will be instrumental in understanding the current lawsuit against the hospital board of directors.

The clause, known as the "ERISA" provision, shifts the liability for future lawsuits from Uber to the hospital board of directors at the facility where the injured parties received treatment. This move is seen as a strategic attempt to deflect the focus away from the technology giant and onto the hospital's administrative decisions.

Hospital Board Liability Under ERISA: A Nuanced Explanation

So, what exactly is the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), and how does it apply to hospital boards in cases like these? Simply put, ERISA regulates employee benefits, including health insurance and retirement plans. However, it also introduces a critical concept known as ERISA preemption, which shields hospital boards from state or local laws governing employer liability.

According to various sources, including a 2020 report from the American Hospital Association, ERISA preemption may limit hospital liability in certain cases. Nevertheless, there are situations where hospital boards can be held accountable under ERISA, primarily when failing to provide adequate care to patients.

A Potential Precedent in Litigation: What Does This Mean for Future Lawsuits?

The 2020 Boston Medical Center Case: A Similar Precedent-Setting Situation

In 2020, a group of patients sued Boston Medical Center, alleging inadequate care during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic. According to a report by NPR, the lawsuit accused the hospital of prioritizing profits over patient care and cited ERISA preemption as a contributing factor in the facility's alleged negligence.

Implications for Patient Care, Hospital Administration, and Liability Landscape

As the case against the Boston Medical Center demonstrates, the precedent set by such lawsuits can have significant implications for patient care and hospital administration. If hospital boards are held liable for inadequate care under ERISA, this may lead to increased scrutiny of their decision-making processes and potentially more comprehensive safety protocols.

Conclusion and Future Implications

While the outcome of the current lawsuit against the hospital board of directors remains to be seen, the move has raised important questions about liability and accountability in the healthcare system. As we continue to navigate the complexities of corporate settlements and institutional liability, this case may set a crucial precedent for future lawsuits against hospital boards.

The long-term implications of this case will undoubtedly have a ripple effect on the healthcare industry, affecting not only patient care but also hospital administration and liability landscape. One thing is certain: the nuances of corporate settlements and ERISA provisions will require continued examination to ensure that the rights of injured parties are adequately protected.
