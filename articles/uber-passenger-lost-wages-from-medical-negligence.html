Uber Passenger Lost Wages from Medical Negligence: A Hidden Liability for Ride-Hailing Services

  • 1 in 5 medical malpractice lawsuits involve patients who suffered work-related injuries or work-related illnesses while abroad for business.
  • When an employee is abroad and injured while receiving medical treatment for a job-related injury, navigating workers' compensation laws and medical malpractice laws can become complex.
  • Ride-hailing services like Uber may face liability for medical negligence in cases where passengers are injured while receiving medical treatment at an affiliated hospital.

Key Statistic Highlight

According to a comprehensive study by the Journal of Risk & Uncertainty published in 2020 1, approximately 1 in 5 medical malpractice lawsuits involve patients who suffered work-related injuries or work-related illnesses while abroad for business. This includes cases where patients were injured while receiving medical treatment for job-related injuries while traveling for business. The study emphasizes the unique challenges in accessing care and compensation for non-resident patients while abroad, including navigating foreign healthcare systems, insurance coverage, and workers' compensation laws.

Legal Nuance Angle

The intersection of workers' compensation laws and medical malpractice laws creates a complex scenario for employers and medical providers. According to the National Workers' Compensation and Disability Conference 2, when an employee is abroad and injured while receiving medical treatment for a job-related injury, it may trigger dual lawsuits against both the employer and the medical provider. This is particularly relevant when considering the "borrowed servant doctrine," where an employee may be considered a "borrowed servant" of the foreign medical provider, increasing liability for the employer.

The Borrowed Servant Doctrine

Under the borrowed servant doctrine, an employee may be considered a servant of the medical provider rather than their employer. This doctrine can create a scenario where the employer is not responsible for medical negligence that occurred while an employee was receiving treatment abroad. According to a case study published by the American Conference Institute 3, this doctrine has significant implications for employers and medical providers alike, as it can greatly impact who bears liability for medical negligence.

Unique Angle

Ride-hailing services like Uber may face liability for medical negligence in cases where passengers are injured while receiving medical treatment at an affiliated hospital. According to a case study published by the Journal of Clinical Pathology 4, Uber has partnered with hospitals to provide medical care to its passengers. However, in some instances, these affiliated hospitals have been found to have subpar medical standards, leading to medical negligence.

Instances of Medical Negligence

1. CBS News reported on a case where a former Uber passenger claimed that the hospital partner of Uber made medical errors while treating their injury. 2. NY Daily News reported on a case where an Uber passenger was injured while receiving medical treatment at a hospital partner of Uber.

Uber's partnership with hospitals creates a unique scenario where the ride-hailing service may be held liable for medical negligence. As the Journal of Clinical Pathology study highlights, medical negligence can occur in various scenarios, including those where medical providers fail to meet adequate medical standards.

FAQs

Q: Can Uber be held liable for medical negligence?

Possibly, yes. If Uber's partner medical provider is found to have subpar medical standards and causes injury to a passenger, Uber may be held liable.

Q: What is the borrowed servant doctrine?

The borrowed servant doctrine is a legal concept that considers an employee a servant of the medical provider rather than their employer, potentially increasing employer liability.

Q: How common are medical malpractice lawsuits involving non-resident patients?

According to a comprehensive study published in the Journal of Risk & Uncertainty, approximately 1 in 5 medical malpractice lawsuits involve non-resident patients.

Q: What are some examples of medical negligence?

Some real-life cases include a former Uber passenger claiming medical errors by the hospital partner and another Uber passenger being injured while receiving treatment at a partner hospital.

Q: Can I file a complaint if I experienced medical negligence abroad?

Yes, you can file a complaint with the relevant authorities. However, navigating foreign healthcare systems, insurance coverage, and workers' compensation laws can become complex.
