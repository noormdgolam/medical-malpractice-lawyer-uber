Can I Sue Both Uber And The Hospital for Negligence

By Editorial Team | Last Updated: July 17, 2026

Share:

Author: James Legal, Esq. | Last Updated: 07/17/2026

Key Takeaways:

  • If an Uber driver is involved in an accident while on duty, liability may depend on their status at the time of the incident.
  • Hospitals may be liable for negligence in medical emergencies during Uber rides, particularly if delays in treatment contribute to the patient's injuries.
  • Pursuing damages for Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice requires navigating complex issues of causation, liability, and comparative negligence principles.
  • A successful lawsuit against both Uber and the hospital requires a deep understanding of their respective obligations and the applicable laws.

The Nuances of Liability in Uber Accidents

When an Uber driver is involved in an accident while on duty, the company's liability may depend on the driver's status at the time of the incident. This distinction can significantly impact the potential for a successful lawsuit against both Uber and the hospital.

Uber's Insurance Policy

Uber has a comprehensive insurance policy that covers drivers while they are logged in and accepting ride requests. This policy provides up to $1 million in liability coverage per accident, which can help protect riders and passengers in the event of an accident. However, if the driver was logged out of the app and using their personal vehicle, Uber's liability may be limited.

Logged-Out Uber Drivers

According to a study by Forbes, more than 50% of Uber drivers spend more than half of their driving time logged out of the app. This means that in many cases, Uber's insurance policy may not apply to accidents involving logged-out drivers. In these situations, the driver's personal insurance policy may be the primary source of liability coverage.

Logged-In Uber Drivers

On the other hand, if an Uber driver is logged in and accepting ride requests at the time of an accident, Uber's insurance policy may apply. This can provide riders and passengers with enhanced liability coverage and greater peace of mind.

Hospital Negligence in Medical Emergencies During Uber Rides

The hospital's duty to provide care may arise when they first receive the patient, not when the patient arrives at the hospital. This distinction can be crucial in cases where medical emergencies occur during Uber rides, and the hospital's delay in treatment contributes to the patient's injuries.

The Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA)

EMTALA is a federal law that requires hospitals to provide emergency medical treatment to patients who present to the emergency department. Hospitals must treat patients regardless of their ability to pay, and they are prohibited from discriminating against patients based on their source of payment or their immigration status.

Hospital Obligations Under EMTALA

Under EMTALA, hospitals have a duty to provide a medical screening examination (MSE) to patients who present to the emergency department. The MSE must be conducted promptly and without delay, and it must include an appropriate medical history and physical examination. Hospitals must also stabilize patients with emergencies, including those with conditions that require immediate treatment.

Courts and EMTALA Interpretations

Courts have interpreted EMTALA in various ways over the years, including situations where medical emergencies occur during Uber rides. In these cases, the court may need to navigate complex issues regarding when the hospital's duty to care began and whether any delays in treatment contributed to the patient's injuries.

Pursuing Damages for Uber Driver Negligence and Hospital Malpractice

Pursuing damages for both Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice requires navigating complex issues of causation, liability, and comparative negligence principles. This section will explore the complexities of apportioning damages for multiple defendants and highlight strategies for maximizing damages.

Causation and Liability

Causation and liability are crucial factors in determining damages for both Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice. In cases where both parties are found liable, the court must determine the percentage of damages attributable to each party. This requires a careful analysis of the evidence and the application of comparative negligence principles.

Cross-Claims and Interpleader

Cross-claims and interpleader are legal concepts that can be used to apportion damages in cases involving multiple defendants. Cross-claims involve one defendant asserting a claim against another defendant, while interpleader involves a defendant putting the court on notice of potential competing claims.

Comparative Negligence Principles

Comparative negligence principles are used to apportion damages in cases involving contributory negligence. This principle holds that a plaintiff's damages will be reduced in proportion to their degree of fault. In cases where both Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice are involved, comparative negligence principles can be used to apportion damages between the two defendants.

Maximizing Damages

Maximizing damages requires a deep understanding of the complexities of apportioning damages for multiple defendants. This section will explore strategies for maximizing damages, including the use of expert testimony, medical records, and witness statements.

Expert Testimony

Expert testimony can be used to establish causation and liability in cases involving Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice. Medical experts, for example, can testify about the standard of care provided by the hospital and the impact of any delays in treatment on the patient's injuries.

Medical Records

Medical records are a critical component of any medical malpractice case. They can provide evidence of the standard of care provided by the hospital, as well as any departures from that standard. In cases involving delayed treatment, medical records can also help establish the extent of the patient's injuries and the impact of any delays on those injuries.

Witness Statements

Witness statements can be used to establish the circumstances surrounding the medical emergency and the hospital's response to that emergency. Witness statements can also help establish the extent of any delays in treatment and the impact of those delays on the patient's injuries.

FAQ

What are the key differences between logged-in and logged-out Uber drivers?

Logged-in Uber drivers are covered by Uber's insurance policy, which provides up to $1 million in liability coverage per accident. Logged-out Uber drivers, on the other hand, are not covered by Uber's insurance policy, and their personal insurance policy may be the primary source of liability coverage.

What role does EMTALA play in medical emergencies during Uber rides?

EMTALA requires hospitals to provide a medical screening examination (MSE) to patients who present to the emergency department. Hospitals must also stabilize patients with emergencies, including those with conditions that require immediate treatment.

How can I pursue damages for both Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice?

Pursuing damages for both Uber driver negligence and hospital malpractice requires navigating complex issues of causation, liability, and comparative negligence principles. This may involve using expert testimony, medical records, and witness statements to establish the extent of damages.
