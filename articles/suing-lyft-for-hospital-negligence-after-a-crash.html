Suing Lyft For Hospital Negligence After A Crash

By Editorial Team | Last Updated: July 17, 2026

Share:

Author: James Legal, Esq. | Last Updated: 07/17/2026

Key Takeaways

  • Suing Lyft for hospital negligence after a crash involves complexities surrounding their insurance coverage and contingency thresholds.
  • Timely medical attention is crucial for the severity and liability of a claim, with delays potentially leading to increased complications or mortality rates.
  • New York's No-Fault automobile insurance law applies to claims against Lyft, potentially limiting recovery for hospital negligence.

Understanding the Liability Maze: Lyft's Insurance and Hospital Oversight

Lyft's insurance coverage plays a significant role in determining the liability maze for crash victims seeking compensation for hospital negligence. The company offers $250,000 in contingent bodily injury liability (CBIL) per person, up to $500,000 per accident. However, the threshold of $250,000 can be a barrier for claimants seeking compensation above this amount.

Hospitals can be held liable for negligence under federal law, particularly the Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA). EMTALA ensures that patients receive necessary medical treatment without regard to insurance status or ability to pay.

Prioritizing Lyft over hospital staff, judges could assign vicarious liability to the company, making them responsible for any damages resulting from the crash and subsequent hospital treatment.

Lyft's Contingent Bodily Injury Liability (CBIL) Coverage

  1. The CBIL threshold of $250,000 may fall short for claimants with severe injuries requiring extensive treatment, leaving them to pursue additional compensation through other means.
  2. The liability coverage applies only when all other sources of coverage have been exhausted.
  3. Lyft's CBIL coverage typically has higher deductibles ($250-$500 per claim) and a higher annual limit ($500,000). This coverage is intended to supplement, rather than replace, other insurance plans.

For instance, a victim of a crash with Lyft may have $250,000 covered by the CBIL policy. However, if their medical expenses exceed $500,000 due to extensive treatments and hospital stays, they will have to rely on other sources, such as underinsured/uninsured motorist coverage or third-party liability insurance.

Hospital Oversight and Liability Under EMTALA

  1. Hospitals must provide a medical screening examination within the emergency department (ED) to determine whether an individual has an emergency medical condition (EMC).
  2. If an EMC is identified, the hospital must stabilize the individual or provide arrangements for further transfer to a capable facility.
  3. Failing to abide by these regulations may incur significant penalties and fines, which are a factor in calculating the total damages in court.

The Critical Role of Prompt Medical Attention in Severity and Liability

Prompt medical attention significantly impacts the severity and liability of a claim in three key ways:

1. Reduced Injuries

Seeking medical attention promptly minimizes the risk of exacerbating the injury, thereby limiting the severity of the damage and the total liability.

2. Optimal Treatment Timing

A delayed response can prolong the patient's condition, potentially leading to long-term health complications and a higher liability for medical expenses.

3. Liability Determination

In cases where medical professionals fail to provide timely or adequate treatment, judges can assign more substantial damages for the patient's suffering and expenses resulting from the delay.

A study by the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons revealed that delayed treatment can lead to severe complications, including infection, increased bleeding, and even mortality. According to research in clinical infectious diseases, prompt treatment significantly improves patients' chances of recovery from infection-related complications.

New York's No-Fault Law and Its Impact on Suing Lyft for Hospital Negligence

  1. The New York Automobile Insurance Law (NYAIL) establishes a system of no-fault insurance, which requires policyholders to file claims under their own insurance policies, excluding third parties, for the initial costs arising from injuries.
  2. The 'basic economic loss rule' prevents a claimant from bringing a lawsuit based solely on an injury sustained in an accident unless there is proof that the other party breached a non-contractual duty, demonstrating a clear breach and significant injury.
  3. Certain cases of reckless conduct or gross negligence can lead to an exception to the basic economic loss rule, allowing claimants to pursue damages from the party responsible for the accident.

To navigate these complexities, litigants may focus on proving the gross negligence of Lyft staff, which can demonstrate a clear breach of the duty of care and potentially create an exception under New York's no-fault law.
