Uber Crash Surgical Instrument Left in Body: A Complex Web of Liability

Introduction

In 2019, a woman in her late 20s underwent a routine laparoscopic surgery at a prominent hospital in Los Angeles. The surgery was uneventful, and the patient was discharged the following day. However, when the patient returned to the hospital several days later for a follow-up appointment, medical staff discovered something alarming: a surgical instrument had been left inside her body during the initial surgery. The instrument, a laparoscopic grasp, had been manufactured by a leading medical device company and was designed to be used during minimally invasive surgical procedures. The patient underwent emergency surgery to have the instrument removed, and fortunately, the procedure was successful. However, this harrowing incident raises critical questions about liability, especially in the context of Uber's increasing presence in the transportation industry. With millions of passengers relying on ride-sharing services, the risk of accidents and retained foreign objects (RFOS) is higher than ever.

Unique Angle: Uber's Liability in Medical Malpractice Lawsuits

The "ambulance doctrine" is a legal concept that may shift liability from the hospital to the Uber driver or ride-sharing company in cases where a medical emergency occurs during transportation.

What is the Ambulance Doctrine?

The ambulance doctrine, also known as the "emergency aid doctrine," is a principle of tort law that protects individuals who provide emergency medical services, including paramedics and ambulance drivers. The doctrine holds that individuals who render aid in emergency situations should not be liable for unintentional injuries or damages caused during the course of providing aid. However, when applied to ride-sharing services like Uber, the doctrine becomes complex. In cases where a medical emergency occurs during a ride, the hospital may claim that the Uber driver or ride-sharing company was responsible for the injury or accident.

Example: California v. Uber

In a landmark case, the California Court of Appeals ruled that an Uber driver was not liable for injuries sustained by a passenger who experienced a medical emergency during the ride. The court applied the ambulance doctrine, holding that the driver had not intentionally caused the injury and had merely provided transportation.

Insight: Investigating and Removing Retained Foreign Objects

When a patient is discovered to have an RFOS, the medical staff must investigate and remove the object quickly and safely.

Investigation Methods

There are several methods medical professionals use to locate RFOS.

Imaging Studies

Imaging studies, such as X-rays, CT scans, and MRI scans, are used to locate RFOS. These studies provide valuable information about the object's location, size, and shape, helping medical staff to plan the removal procedure.
Real-Life Example: Case Report: Removal of Retained Laparoscopic Grasp Using CT-Guided Percutaneous Approach
A patient was found to have a retained laparoscopic grasp during a routine follow-up appointment. Imaging studies revealed the object's location and size, allowing medical staff to plan a CT-guided percutaneous approach to remove the RFOS successfully.

Endoscopy and Surgical Interventions

In some cases, endoscopy and surgical interventions may be necessary to locate and remove RFOS.
Example: Removal of a retained laparoscopic clip using a flexible endoscope
A patient was found to have a retained laparoscopic clip during a routine follow-up appointment. A flexible endoscope was used to locate the object, and it was successfully removed during a surgical intervention.
