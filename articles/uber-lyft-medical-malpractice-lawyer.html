How Uber & Lyft Accident Injuries Become Medical Malpractice Cases

By Editorial Team | Last Updated: October 25, 2023

Key Takeaways

  • Rideshare accidents can trigger two separate claims: personal injury against the driver/company, and medical malpractice against negligent healthcare providers.
  • Surgical errors, misdiagnosis, or delayed treatment in the ER after an Uber/Lyft crash are common grounds for malpractice.
  • Dual-liability claims require specialized attorneys who understand both personal injury auto laws and complex medical malpractice statutes.
  • Uber and Lyft's $1 million insurance policies may not cover damages caused by a third-party doctor's negligence.

The Intersection of Rideshare Accidents and Hospital Negligence

When you are involved in a severe accident while riding in an Uber or Lyft, the immediate focus is naturally on the crash itself. Victims are rushed to the emergency room, often requiring emergency surgery, orthopedic stabilization, or neurological intervention. But what happens when the treatment you receive at the hospital makes your injuries significantly worse?

This is where personal injury law intersects with medical malpractice. If a doctor, nurse, or hospital facility fails to adhere to the accepted standard of medical care while treating your accident-related injuries, you may have grounds for a secondary, independent claim.

Common Scenarios Leading to Malpractice Claims

Not every bad medical outcome is considered malpractice. However, in the chaotic environment of an ER trauma center following an auto accident, devastating mistakes can happen. Some of the most frequent scenarios include:

  • Failure to Diagnose Internal Injuries: After a high-impact T-bone crash, internal bleeding or organ damage might not be immediately obvious. A failure to order the correct CT scans or MRIs can lead to a wrongful death or permanent impairment.
  • Surgical Errors: From operating on the wrong site to leaving surgical instruments inside the body during emergency trauma surgery.
  • Medication Errors: Administering incorrect dosages of painkillers or anesthesia, particularly when the patient's medical history (like allergies) was ignored.
  • Premature Discharge: Releasing an accident victim before they are medically stable, leading to severe complications at home.

Navigating Dual-Liability: Who Pays?

One of the most complex aspects of these cases is determining liability and sourcing compensation. Uber and Lyft both carry $1,000,000 commercial liability insurance policies that apply when a driver is engaged in a ride. If the driver is at fault, this policy covers your initial accident injuries.

However, that $1 million policy will not cover the exacerbated injuries caused by a negligent surgeon. For those damages, your attorney must file a separate medical malpractice claim against the doctor's or hospital's malpractice insurance carrier. This requires parsing exactly which damages stem from the crash, and which stem from the medical error.

Why You Need a Specialized Attorney

Handling a standard auto accident claim is vastly different from litigating a medical malpractice suit. Malpractice cases require extensive expert testimony, affidavits of merit, and adherence to strict pre-suit requirements. An attorney attempting to juggle both claims must be deeply versed in both fields of law.

According to data from the Bureau of Justice Statistics, medical malpractice cases are among the most vigorously defended civil suits, with doctors winning a significant majority of cases that go to trial. You need legal representation that can build an airtight case from the start.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Can I sue both the Uber driver and the hospital?

Yes. If the driver caused the accident and the hospital provided substandard care that worsened your condition, you can pursue claims against both parties simultaneously.

What is the statute of limitations?

The statute of limitations varies by state. In many states, you have 2 to 3 years for personal injury, but medical malpractice limits can be shorter (e.g., 1 to 2 years from the date the malpractice was discovered).
