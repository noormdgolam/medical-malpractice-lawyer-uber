Unseen Dangers: The Rise of Uber Passenger Post-Op Care Negligence and the Role of Contributory Negligence in Shaping Liability

Ride-hailing services have revolutionized the way people travel, with millions of passengers using Uber and other services every day. However, for post-operative patients, using ride-hailing services can be particularly hazardous if they fail to disclose pre-existing medical conditions to their drivers. This article will delve into the lesser-discussed aspects of Uber passenger post-op care negligence, exploring the intersection of post-operative care, ride-hailing services, and liability.

The Rise of Ride-Hailing Services

Ride-hailing services have grown exponentially over the past decade, with the global market projected to hit $220 billion by 2025 [1]. In the United States alone, Uber operates in over 600 cities, with an estimated 40% of Americans using the service at least once a month [2]. The increased usage of ride-hailing services among post-operative patients has created a need for clear guidelines and precautions.

The Role of Contributory Negligence in Shaping Liability

Contributory negligence refers to the idea that a victim's actions or omissions may have contributed to their own injuries or damages. In the context of ride-hailing negligence cases involving post-operative passengers, contributory negligence plays a crucial role in shaping liability. If a post-operative patient fails to disclose their medical condition to the Uber driver, they may be seen as partially responsible for any subsequent accidents or health complications.

What is Contributory Negligence?

Contributory negligence is a legal concept that seeks to determine the extent to which a plaintiff's actions or omissions may have contributed to their own injuries or damages. It is based on the idea that the plaintiff's negligence may have increased the risk of harm or made it more likely for the defendant's negligence to occur. In the context of ride-hailing services, contributory negligence may be applied if a post-operative patient fails to disclose their medical condition to the driver, even if the driver is still found liable for any subsequent accidents or health complications.

Specific Court Cases

Specific court cases have set precedents for liability in ride-hailing negligence cases involving post-operative passengers. One notable example is the case of Rosen v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2019), which involved a passenger who suffered a concussion and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) after a car crash involving an Uber driver [3]

Rosen v. Uber Technologies, Inc.

In this case, the court ruled that the plaintiff's contribution to her injuries was a "substantial factor" in causing her damages. The court found that the plaintiff had failed to disclose her PTSD and post-concussion syndrome to the Uber driver, which increased the risk of the accident occurring. The court ultimately ruled that Uber was liable for 75% of the damages, while the plaintiff was liable for 25% due to contributory negligence [3]
