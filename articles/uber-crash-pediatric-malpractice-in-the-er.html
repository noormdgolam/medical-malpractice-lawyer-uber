Expert Analysis of Liability: Unpacking the Common Carrier Doctrine

Ride-sharing services like Uber have transformed the way people move around cities, but with the increased freedom comes a new set of liability concerns. In the event of an Uber crash, questions arise about who is responsible for the damages or injuries suffered by the passengers, particularly in cases involving minors.

Hidden Dangers of Pediatric Malpractice: Statistics and Trends

Pediatric malpractice cases in emergency ER settings are a pressing concern, with alarming statistics revealing a disproportionate impact on minority and low-income communities. A study by the American Academy of Pediatrics found that African American children are 6 times more likely to die from preventable injuries than their white counterparts, while Hispanic children are 3 times more likely.

A study published in the Journal of Health Care for the Poor and Underserved reveals that low-income children are more likely to experience adverse outcomes in ER settings, including increased morbidity and mortality rates.

Case Study: Hassan vs. Uber Technologies, Inc.

The 2021 case of Hassan vs. Uber Technologies, Inc. may set a new precedent for ER liability in Uber crash cases involving minors. The case involved a 5-year-old boy who suffered serious injuries in an Uber crash and received subpar care in the ER, resulting in permanent injuries.

The court ultimately ruled in favor of the family, finding that Uber had a duty of care towards the minor passenger and was liable for the damages suffered as a result of the ER malpractice.

This case highlights the importance of understanding the common carrier doctrine and its implications for ride-sharing services like Uber in pediatric ER settings.
